Today Pussel answers "where in the picture does this piece go?" by matching surface texture (SIFT→NCC hybrid, 76.7% on real photos). 1 · Why geometry is worth a column

The texture matcher answers a global question (position in the finished picture) and needs the box-lid overview photo to do it. Geometry answers a local question — which pieces physically interlock — and needs nothing but the pieces themselves. That makes it useful in situations texture can't reach:

  • Sky and solid-color regions where texture matching has nothing to grip — shape complementarity still works. (Existence proof: Stuff Made Here's robot assembled a 4000-piece single-color puzzle on shape alone.)
  • Neighbor suggestions: "these 3 pieces likely attach to the piece you just placed" — shape narrows candidates, texture/color across the seam confirms.
  • Unique piece identity: a persistent fingerprint (shape + surface color) lets the app remember pieces across frames and across sessions — the foundation for the "scan pieces one by one, mark them green" camera experience.
  • A second, independent signal to fuse with the texture matcher — the two fail in different ways, so combining them should beat either alone.

3 · What the field says (research findings)

The consistent recipe across every working real-photo solver

Independent projects (Wolfson et al. 1988 — the 104-piece original; kellinwood/PuzzleSolver; Zolver; byWulf/jigsawlutioner; several writeups) converge on essentially the same pipeline, which is strong evidence it's the right default:

  • Capture is everything. Every successful project controls the background — flatbed scanner with lid open, black mat, or a backlit lightbox. Segmentation is then trivial thresholding. The capture setup, not the algorithm, determines whether downstream steps work.
  • Corner detection is the fragile step. Every project either documents failure modes or ships a manual-correction UI. Approaches: approxPolyDP, Harris corners + "most rectangular 4-subset" selection, Shi-Tomasi with distance constraints, curvature extrema along the boundary. Plan for occasional human-in-the-loop correction.
  • Tab/blank/flat classification is considered solved. Draw the chord between adjacent corners; if the contour midpoint bulges away from the centroid → tab, toward it → blank, on the chord → flat. Multiple independent implementations, no reported trouble.
  • Edge matching = arc-length resample + chamfer-style distance. Normalize each side (translate/rotate to axis-aligned), resample to N equidistant points (~100), compare a side against a flipped candidate side by summed closest-point distance. Some projects use ~6 hand-engineered scalars instead (protrusion width/depth/position) — cheaper, more interpretable, hashable.

Findings that shape our design

  • Shape alone is not a unique fingerprint on most puzzles. jigsawlutioner's operational finding: geometric matching works reliably only on Ravensburger; most manufacturers reuse near-identical die-cut tab shapes across many pieces. Color/texture disambiguation is mandatory for identity — geometry proposes, appearance confirms. This validates the vision's instinct to combine both.
  • Perspective must be corrected in software. Hobby projects dodge it with scanners; Stuff Made Here dodged it with a telecentric lens. For a phone, a known mat (ArUco/ChArUco fiducials) gives both metric scale (px→mm) and a homography to rectify tilt. A few degrees of uncorrected tilt distorts tab geometry enough to break matching.
  • ARKit/LiDAR depth is the wrong tool — ~1–3 cm accuracy vs. sub-millimeter-relevant tab features. Use 2D + fiducial. ARKit is still useful as capture guidance (hold flat, hold steady).
  • On-device contouring is available for free: Apple's VNDetectContoursRequest (iOS 14+) returns outline paths in real time and is explicitly tuned for high-contrast scenes — which our controlled-mat capture provides anyway. Deep segmentation (MobileSAM/FastSAM via Core ML) is the fallback for uncontrolled backgrounds, not the starting point.
  • No prior art found for persistent cross-session piece fingerprinting — the closest analogue is person/vehicle re-identification (feature vector + nearest-neighbor with threshold, never exact hash equality). This part of the vision is a genuine gap/opportunity.
The one hard constraint to internalize Everything downstream of capture degrades together: shadows read as contour, tilt warps tabs, motion blur kills corners. The cheapest, highest-leverage "algorithm" in this whole column is a defined capture protocol — a plain mat (possibly printed with fiducials), near-overhead phone, diffuse light. Milestone 1 exists to establish exactly this before any clever code.

4 · The proposed pipeline

One frame (or photo) in, one structured piece record out:

1
Capture
Controlled mat, near-overhead. Optional ArUco corners for scale + tilt.
2
Rectify
Homography from fiducials (or skip in v1: assume flat overhead).
3
Segment + contour
rembg / threshold / VNDetectContours → one closed, full-res contour.
4
Find 4 corners
Curvature extrema / Harris + rectangular-subset. The fragile step.
5
Split + classify
4 edge segments; chord-midpoint test → tab / blank / flat.
6
Describe
Per edge: resampled polyline + fitted TabParameters (our 11-param model).
7
Fingerprint
Rotation-invariant shape descriptor + color histogram / patch embedding.
8
Match
Identity: nearest-neighbor vs. seen pieces. Fit: edge vs. inverted candidate edges.

The piece record (sketch)

Each scanned piece becomes a small JSON document — this is what gets stored, deduplicated against, and matched:

  • contour: full-res closed polygon (px), plus mm scale if fiducials present
  • corners: 4 indices into the contour + confidence
  • edges[4]: type (tab/blank/flat), normalized 100-point polyline, fitted TabParameters, fit residual
  • appearance: color histogram (LAB) + small patch embedding of the piece face
  • fingerprint: rotation-invariant combination of the above (canonical edge ordering, e.g. start from lexicographically smallest edge descriptor)
  • quality: sharpness, contour smoothness, corner confidence — gates "locked" status in the live UX
Why fit TabParameters at all, when a resampled polyline suffices for matching? Three reasons: (1) it's a 11-number semantic compression — cheap to store, index, and hash; (2) the fit residual is a built-in quality score ("this contour doesn't look like a puzzle edge" → bad scan, don't lock); (3) it closes the loop with the synthetic training pipeline — real fitted parameter distributions can drive more realistic synthetic data (feeding back into the first column). The polyline stays alongside as ground truth; the parameters are the summary.

6 · Milestones

Ordered so each one is small, independently valuable, and produces a number or a demo — never "infrastructure only". Phases A–B are pure Python against existing photos (no new hardware, no iOS work); the camera experience comes last, once the algorithm is proven.

Phase A · Offline geometry extraction (Python, north_star photos)

Prove we can turn an existing real photo into four labeled edges. Everything runs as scripts in network/experiments/, evaluated with contact sheets like exp25.

M1 High-fidelity contour extraction DONE ✓

~2–4 sessions · builds on piece_detector.py + background_remover.py
  • Upgrade the existing rembg pipeline from "≤60-point UI polygon" to a full-resolution, smoothed, closed contour (mask → morphological cleanup → findContours → light smoothing).
  • Run over all 236 north_star pieces × 4 backgrounds; render overlay contact sheets for eyeball review.
  • Note which backgrounds fail (the red carpet will be the stress test) — this defines the capture protocol for the eventual scanning mat.
≥95% of pieces yield a single clean closed contour on at least the 2 friendly backgrounds; failure modes are catalogued.
Criterion met on all four backgrounds, not just two. rembg (u2net) clean rates over 944 photos: gray_fabric 98.7%, red_carpet 97.9%, wood 96.2%, cardboard 95.3%. The Otsu-threshold baseline is dead on textured real backgrounds (0.8–22.5%) — rembg is the extraction method, full stop. Gate-failure taxonomy (28 cases): 17 multi-component segmentations, 8 border-touching crops, 3 implausible shapes. Visual review found what the gate cannot: on wood, a few pieces per sheet pass the gate but the contour follows dark artwork boundaries instead of the piece edge (dark art blends into wood tones); gray fabric and red carpet sheets are essentially flawless, with drop shadows correctly excluded. Capture-protocol consequence: the scanning mat should be neutral gray fabric. These wood "silent failures" resurface as the dominant error source in M3 and M4 — segmentation quality upper-bounds the whole column.

M2 Corner detection bake-off DONE ✓

~3–5 sessions · the known-fragile step, so measure it properly
  • Hand-label 4 corners on a ~60-piece subset (tiny matplotlib click tool — an afternoon).
  • Implement 2–3 candidates: curvature extrema along the contour; Harris/Shi-Tomasi + "most rectangular 4-subset"; approxPolyDP baseline.
  • Benchmark corner error (as % of piece size) per method per background.
Best method places all 4 corners within 3% of piece size on ≥90% of the subset; we know its failure modes well enough to design the manual-nudge fallback.
Criterion met — polydp 98.5% and curvature 97.0% of 200 synthetic pieces (known ground-truth corners, random rotation + blur + noise) within 3% worst-corner error, median ~0.7%. The literature's warning proved exact: naïve subset scoring picked tab-bulb tips (bigger quadrilaterals) — initial rates were just 68% / 29.5%. Three changes fixed it: (1) a cornerness prior — true corners have locally straight contour shoulders on both sides, tab tips curve away immediately (PCA line fits + angle-near-90° score per candidate); (2) quad area normalized by hull area so it can't dominate; (3) corners refined as the intersection of the shoulder lines, recovering the un-rounded corner that corner_radius hides (p97 error 3.6% → 0.8%). Shi-Tomasi is structurally unusable (13.5%): goodFeaturesToTrack never even proposes rounded true corners (~33% missing from its pool). On real photos the corner overlays look excellent; synthetic random pieces are harder than real ones (more extreme tabs), so these numbers are conservative. Quantitative real-photo eval awaits optional hand labels (label_corners.py click tool is ready). Working recipe: polydp primary, curvature as cross-check — their disagreement (>3% of bbox diagonal) is the per-piece quality flag.

M3 Edge splitting + tab/blank/flat classification DONE ✓

~1–2 sessions · literature says this is the easy part
  • Split contour at corners into 4 arcs; chord-midpoint-vs-centroid test per arc.
  • Ground truth is nearly free: north_star grid positions tell us which edges are flat (border pieces), and interlocking neighbors constrain tab/blank parity — hand-verify the remainder on contact sheets.
≥98% edge-type accuracy across all cleanly-contoured pieces. Output: every north_star piece stored as a draft piece record (contour + corners + 4 typed edges).
Criterion met: flat-edge accuracy vs grid ground truth 99.68% (gray_fabric), 98.38% (red_carpet); wood 97.25%, cardboard 98.0%. The chord-deviation distribution is strongly bimodal exactly as the literature promised — flat edges deviate <4% of chord length, tabs/blanks 11–42% — so FLAT_THRESHOLD=0.07 sits in a wide safe gap; classification itself is genuinely the easy step. Neighbor tab↔blank complementarity: 99.4% of adjacent pairs on gray_fabric. Cross-background signature consistency 80.5%, dragged down by two things that are not classifier errors: (1) wood segmentation leaks from M1 corrupting corners (the corner_disagreement flags concentrate exactly there), and (2) toddler puzzles (peppa_kitchen 90.2%, peppa_aquarium 94.1%) have genuinely figural border edges — physically shaped cutouts on "flat" borders, verified visually — so the "border ⇒ flat" ground-truth assumption is what's wrong; standard die-cut puzzles score 99.5–100%. Output achieved: 916 piece records (corners + 4 typed edges + 100-pt polyline per edge), 0 splitting failures.

M4 Edge complementarity matching — the first payoff DONE ✓

~3–4 sessions · the milestone that proves the column's core value
  • Normalize each edge (rotate to axis, arc-length resample to 100 points); match edge A against flipped edge B via chamfer distance. Also try the ~6-scalar descriptor variant.
  • Evaluation is beautiful here: north_star grid positions give every true neighbor pair. For each interior edge, rank all candidate mating edges in the same puzzle — where does the true neighbor rank?
Report: true mating edge ranked #1 for X% of edges, top-5 for Y% — per puzzle brand. This number tells us how strong the geometry signal is on our actual puzzles (the jigsawlutioner brand-dependence question, answered with our data).
Reported: true mate top-1 = 67.4%, top-3 = 85.2%, top-5 = 90.5% over 1,878 interior-edge queries against ~25-candidate pools (all type-compatible edges of other pieces, no orientation leak); gray_fabric top-1 = 77.4%, and median rank of the true mate is 1 on every gray_fabric puzzle. Metric bake-off: plain pointwise L2 on canonical chord-normalized 100-pt polylines wins — chamfer loses (61.9% — arc-length index alignment carries information; nearest-point freedom lets impostors cheat), the 6-scalar descriptor collapses too much shape (29.3%), and the chord-length penalty actively hurts (28.2% — camera distance varies per photo, empirically confirming the scale-free design decision). The corner_disagreement quality gate is worth ~8 top-1 points (67.4% vs 59.4% including flagged records). The mate-flip transform was verified exactly (distance 0.0) on a puzzle_shapes shared-edge pair. The jigsawlutioner question, answered on our data: die-cut shape collisions are real — median best-impostor/true-mate margin is only 1.22×, and failures are visibly near-identical competing tabs, not matcher errors. Geometry is a strong ranker but cannot be a sole accept/reject threshold: color must join the fingerprint (→ M6/M7), exactly as §3 predicted.

Phase B · Piece identity

From "an edge matches an edge" to "I recognize this specific piece" — the prerequisite for the scan-and-lock UX.

M6 Piece fingerprint + re-identification benchmark DONE — MET VIA M7 SCORER

~3–4 sessions · north_star's 4-backgrounds-per-piece design was made for this
  • Fingerprint = rotation-invariant shape descriptor (canonically ordered 4-edge descriptors) + LAB color histogram (+ optionally a small patch embedding).
  • Re-ID protocol: enroll each piece from background 1, query with backgrounds 2–4 (different lighting/surface), nearest-neighbor over all 236 pieces.
  • Ablate: shape only vs. color only vs. combined.
Top-1 re-ID ≥95% combined, with the ablation quantifying how much each signal contributes.
Criterion not met on the deliberately-harsh cross-background benchmark: 91.5% top-1 / 97.5% top-5 (enroll on one background, query on the other three, 236-piece cross-puzzle gallery, hyperparameters frozen on validation cells only; progression across three iterations 81.5% → 86.1% → 91.5%). The ablation was full of lessons: fusion style matters more than features — a z-normalized linear blend degenerated to shape-only even though failures were gross color mismatches, while reciprocal-rank fusion worked; gray-world normalization more than doubled color-only accuracy (22.8% → 50.7%); and the milestone's discovery is the 3×3 spatial color layout, which alone beats shape (85.2% vs 81.5%) and dissolves same-palette sibling confusion — RRF(shape, spatial) reached 94.3%. The entire remaining gap is wood-query capture quality (underexposed, warm-cast photos of dark artwork): excluding wood queries, cells run 94.8–97.5%. Within-puzzle ("which of my 24 pieces is this"): up to 96.2% top-1 / 99.6% top-5. Production outlook: the app's same-mat, same-session scenario is strictly easier than every benchmarked cell — ≥95% looks realistic on the gray mat, but proving it needs a same-background repeat-capture set. Fix path: capture-time exposure/WB control, not more descriptor engineering. Addendum from M7: the distance-space z-sum scorer built for thresholding turned out to also be the best retriever — 95.2% top-1 on the same leakage-free cells (independently verified), retroactively meeting the ≥95% criterion; non-wood queries run 96.3–96.9%.

M7 Uniqueness / collision study DONE ✓

~1–2 sessions · mostly analysis on M6's data
  • Genuine-vs-impostor similarity distributions; per-brand shape collision rates (do our kids' puzzles reuse die-cut shapes like jigsawlutioner warns?).
  • Choose the "locked" confidence threshold for the live UX from these curves.
A documented threshold with expected false-match / false-new-piece rates.
Criterion met — a documented two-threshold recipe with measured error rates, plus two findings that reshape the column. The recipe: score z = z_shape + z_spatial (each z-normalized by the enroll gallery's own impostor statistics, which proved nearly identical across galleries — so one threshold transfers). Frozen on validation cells, measured on the 9 leakage-free cells: auto-lock at z < −4.78 (0.19% wrong-lock, 1.08% false-merge of new pieces), declare-new at z > −0.80 (0.89% false-new), gray zone in between (~26–30% of genuine re-scans at strict settings; relaxing to the FMR=1% point cuts that to ~8% — a product decision, both points on the shipped ROC; EER 2.29%). Finding #1: zero full-piece die-cut collisions across all 1,695 distinct within-puzzle pairs — every nearest pair sits 1.1–3.4× above the "same piece re-photographed" bar. Single edges collide (M4); all four edges together never did on these 14 puzzles — piece uniqueness is not the bottleneck. Finding #2: the z-sum is also the best retriever (95.2% top-1 on the leakage-free cells, independently verified) — one score serves both ranking and thresholding, retiring M6's rank-fusion machinery and retroactively meeting M6's ≥95% criterion.

The E2E again caught what unit tests couldn't: the backend's uncertain status has a second, fingerprint-less flavor (contour quality gate failed — z_score = null) for which the confirm chip would be a dead end (on_uncertain=enroll has nothing to enroll); the controller now maps it to a distinct "couldn't read the piece" verdict that just keeps scanning.

Verified on the physical iPhone 15 with real pieces — and the device run drove three same-day product fixes: (1) a stale (backend-restarted) puzzle id now self-heals — the scanner's 404 triggers a re-upload and one retry, watched live as 404 → upload → lock; (2) enrolled pieces now join the puzzle page's piece list as regular capture entries (position prediction, overlay marker, disk persistence — which is also where gallery thumbnails come back from on later scanner visits), with matched verdicts adding nothing so the list stays duplicate-free; (3) enrollment went hands-free — M7's own measurement (97.9% of new pieces land in the gray zone) meant the confirm chip cost one tap per piece, so t_accept moved to M7's FMR=1% ROC point (−3.98; gray-zone re-scans ~26–30% → ~8% at 1% wrong-lock risk) and gray-zone verdicts auto-enroll. Observed end state on-device: new piece → zero-tap lock → enrolled → predicted → in the piece list; re-pointing at scanned pieces → "Already scanned" with zero duplicates across repeated re-locks. Dark/black surfaces defeat the preview detector (no outline, no lock) exactly as M1 predicted — wood and mid-tones work; the gray mat remains the recommendation. 114 iOS + 212 backend tests pass.

M11 Neighbor suggestions — the two columns meet

~3–5 sessions · first user-visible fusion of geometry + texture
  • For any scanned piece, rank other scanned pieces by edge complementarity (M4 matcher), confirmed by color continuity across the seam; show "likely neighbors" in the gallery.
  • Fuse with the existing texture matcher's position prediction: geometry proposes local fits, texture anchors global position — flag disagreements.
On a scanned 24-piece kids' puzzle, the true neighbor appears in the top-3 suggestions for ≥80% of edges.

7 · Risks & mitigations

RiskLikelihoodMitigation
Corner detection unreliable on real photos (the field's universal pain point) High → retired by M2 Confirmed as the hard step (naïve scoring hit only 29–68%), then solved with the shoulder-straightness cornerness prior + line-intersection refinement: 97–98.5% within 3% error. The polydp/curvature disagreement flag is the per-piece confidence signal (worth ~8 top-1 matching points in M4).
Shape collisions — our puzzle brands reuse die-cut tab shapes, weakening geometry-only matching Confirmed by M4 Median best-impostor margin just 1.22×; failures are visibly near-identical competing tabs. Geometry ranks (top-5 90.5%) but cannot threshold — shape + color fingerprints (M6/M7) carry identity, as planned.
Perspective tilt distorts tab geometry from handheld capture Medium v1 relies on capture guidance (level indicator via device gyro, "hold overhead" UI); printable ArUco mat as the upgrade path if M4 numbers degrade on handheld vs. tripod shots.
Latency of the stream-to-backend loop makes live scanning feel sluggish Medium Hybrid path is designed in: cheap on-device VNDetectContours for the live overlay, backend for the precise lock-time extraction. Decide with real numbers in M9.
north_star photos (upright, arrow marker, curated) are easier than live handheld frames Certain, by design Fine for algorithm development; M10's success criterion is on live physical pieces, and a small "handheld capture" photo set can be added to north_star once the protocol from M1 is settled.
rembg (u2net) too slow or imprecise for full-res contour work Resolved by M1 — inverted rembg is the winner (95–99% clean); Otsu thresholding is unusable on textured real surfaces (0.8–22.5%). Remaining rembg weakness: dark artwork against wood — solved by mat choice (gray fabric), not by algorithm.

8 · Suggested first step

Start with M1 + M2 together as a single experiment (e.g. network/experiments/exp28_piece_geometry/): full-res contours over north_star, corner bake-off with a small hand-labeled set, contact sheets for review. It needs no new photos, no iOS work, no new dependencies, and it retires the two biggest unknowns — contour fidelity and corner reliability — before anything is built on top of them. M4 (neighbor-edge ranking) is then the first moment the whole column proves its worth with one number.

A parallel cheap spike, if appetite allows: a ~1-hour Xcode playground pointing VNDetectContoursRequest at a piece on the gray mat, just to see on-device outline quality and frame rate first-hand. It doesn't block anything, and it informs the M9 build-vs-stream decision early.
